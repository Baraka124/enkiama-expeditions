Practical Tanzania

The unglamorous questions,
answered plainly.

Visas, vaccinations, insurance, what to put in a bag. None of it is romantic, and all of it matters. Here is what we know — and where we would rather you trust a doctor than a travel company.

Do I need a visa?

Most visitors do. Many nationalities can apply online in advance through Tanzania’s official immigration portal, and many can also obtain one on arrival at Kilimanjaro or Dar es Salaam. Requirements differ by passport and change from time to time.

What we do: before anything is booked, we confirm the current position for your specific nationality, and tell you plainly which route we would take in your position — including when applying in advance is simply less stressful than queuing on arrival.

Yellow fever — do I need the certificate?

It depends on where you are travelling from, not only where you are going. Tanzania generally requires proof of yellow fever vaccination if you arrive from, or have recently transited through, a country where yellow fever is a risk. Flying directly from Europe or North America, it is usually not required.

The catch: a transit stop can change the answer. We look at your actual routing, not a generic rule, and tell you what applies to the flights you are actually taking.

What about malaria?

Malaria is present in much of Tanzania, including safari areas, and antimalarial medication is commonly advised. Precautions like repellent, long sleeves at dusk, and treated nets matter too.

Where we stop: this is a medical decision, and we are not doctors. Speak with a travel clinic or your own physician, ideally several weeks before you travel. What we can do is tell you honestly where you will be, at what altitude, and in what season, so that whoever advises you can do it properly.

Do I need travel insurance?

Yes — and we ask every traveller to carry it. Comprehensive cover including medical care and emergency evacuation is not a formality in remote country; it is the difference between an inconvenience and a crisis.

If you are climbing Kilimanjaro: check that your policy covers trekking to the altitude you intend to reach. A great many standard policies quietly stop well below the summit, and travellers discover this at the worst possible moment.

What should I actually pack?

Less than you think. Neutral, comfortable layers; a genuinely warm jacket for early mornings and the crater rim, which surprises people; a hat; sunscreen; binoculars if you own them; and any medication you need. Laundry is available on most journeys, which halves what you carry.

What we send: a packing note written for your route and season — not a generic list. What to bring for October in the Serengeti is not what to bring for February on the mountain’s slopes.

Money, and the question of tipping

The Tanzanian shilling is the local currency, and US dollars are widely accepted for larger payments. Cards work in cities and better lodges; cash still matters in smaller places.

On tipping: it is customary here, and genuinely appreciated. We will tell you honestly what is normal and what it means to the person receiving it, so you can decide for yourself. One thing we will say plainly: because our guides and crews are paid properly to begin with, a tip here is a thank-you — never a substitute for a wage someone should already have received.

Rules change. Nationalities differ. Anything on this page could be out of date by the time it matters to you — so we confirm every detail against your passport and your routing before a single thing is booked.

What we handle, so you do not have to.

  • Confirming visa requirements for your nationality, and advising the least stressful route to one.
  • Checking your flight routing against yellow fever rules — before it becomes a problem at a desk.
  • Telling you exactly where you will be, at what altitude and season, so your doctor can advise you properly.
  • A packing note written for your route, not a generic list.
  • Airport meeting, transfers, and the parts of the day that are easy to get wrong.
  • Being reachable throughout the journey, not only before it.

If a practical question is standing between you and the journey, ask it. We would rather answer it now than have it worry you later.
